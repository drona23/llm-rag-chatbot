FEDERAL STUDENT LOANS - FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

Q: What's the difference between subsidized and unsubsidized loans?
A: Subsidized loans: Government pays interest while you're in school (at least half-time). Interest rate is lower (6.53% in 2024-2025). Unsubsidized loans: You're responsible for all interest from day one. Interest accrues while in school. Interest rate is higher (7.05%). Both have 6-month grace period after graduation.

Q: Can I apply for federal student loans without financial need?
A: Yes, but only for unsubsidized loans and PLUS loans. Subsidized loans require demonstrated financial need. All loans require FAFSA completion to determine need. Financial need = Cost of Attendance minus Expected Family Contribution (EFC).

Q: What's the maximum I can borrow?
A: Depends on your status: Undergrad dependent: $31,000 total (subsidized + unsubsidized). Undergrad independent: $57,500 total. Graduate student: $138,500 total. PLUS loans: Up to cost of attendance (no annual limit). This is per degree level.

Q: What happens if I don't pay back my student loans?
A: Default occurs after 270 days of non-payment. Consequences: Credit score damaged, wage garnishment (up to 25%), tax refund offset, ineligibility for future federal aid, potential legal action. However, many repayment options available if you can't afford payments.

Q: Do I have to start paying back immediately after graduation?
A: No. Federal loans have a 6-month grace period after graduation or dropping below half-time enrollment. PLUS loans begin accruing interest immediately but can be deferred while student is enrolled. Private loans often require payments while in school.

Q: What's the difference between deferment and forbearance?
A: Deferment: Temporarily postpone payments, interest doesn't accrue on subsidized loans (government pays). Must qualify (economic hardship, unemployment, etc.). Forbearance: Temporarily reduce/stop payments, interest accrues on all loans. No income verification needed. Shorter duration than deferment.

Q: Can I get my student loans forgiven?
A: Yes, through several programs: Public Service Loan Forgiveness (10 years), Teacher Loan Forgiveness (up to $17,500), Income-driven repayment forgiveness (20-25 years), Perkins loan forgiveness (profession-dependent). Note: Forgiven amount may be taxable income.

Q: How do income-driven repayment plans work?
A: Your monthly payment is a percentage of your discretionary income (typically 10-20%). Income verified annually through FAFSA. Payment can be as low as $0 if you have very low income. Interest accrues on unsubsidized loans even if payment is low. Remaining balance forgiven after 20-25 years.

Q: Should I consolidate my loans?
A: Consolidation benefits: Single monthly payment, access to some forgiveness programs, can extend repayment. Consolidation drawbacks: Extends repayment (increases total interest), may lose some benefits, interest rate is weighted average (rounded up). Best if you have multiple loans with different interest rates.

Q: What's the difference between federal and private student loans?
A: Federal: Fixed interest rates, income-driven repayment options, loan forgiveness programs, no credit check, borrower protections. Private: Variable interest rates possible, fewer repayment options, no forgiveness programs, credit check required, fewer borrower protections. Private loans are riskier.

Q: Can I deduct student loan interest on my taxes?
A: Yes, up to $2,500 per year as a deduction (not a credit). Income limits apply (starts reducing at $75,000 AGI for single filers, $150,000 for married). Must have paid interest during the year. Doesn't need to be itemizing to claim.

Q: What happens if I go back to school after graduating?
A: If you return to at least half-time enrollment, your grace period on federal loans "resets." You can defer payments while back in school. If you take out additional loans, they're separate. Your earlier loans may still be in repayment if you're only part-time.

Q: Can I pay off my loans early without penalty?
A: Yes. Federal loans have NO prepayment penalty. You can pay extra toward principal anytime. Extra payments reduce total interest paid over time. Recommended if you have high-interest loans or want to be debt-free faster.

Q: What's the 120-payment rule for Public Service Loan Forgiveness?
A: If you work for government/nonprofit and make 120 monthly payments (10 years) on an income-driven plan, remaining balance is forgiven. Payments must be "qualifying payments." Lender must confirm eligibility. Tax consequence: Forgiven amount NOT taxable for PSLF.

Q: Why is my payment higher than expected?
A: Possible reasons: Interest capitalization (unpaid interest added to principal), missed payments (extending repayment period), consolidation loan (longer terms), switched from subsidized to unsubsidized, PLUS loan (highest interest rate at 8.05%), private loans (rates up to 13%+).

Q: Can I get a student loan if I have bad credit?
A: Federal loans: No credit check, bad credit doesn't matter. Private loans: Most require credit check, bad credit may result in denial or higher interest rates. Solution: Get a cosigner (someone with good credit), or focus on federal loans only.

Q: What's the difference between a loan and a grant?
A: Grant: Free money, don't have to repay, based on financial need. Loan: Must repay with interest. Scholarships: Free money, merit-based. Work-study: Part-time job while in school. First apply for grants/scholarships before taking loans.

Q: How long does it take to pay off student loans?
A: Depends on plan: Standard 10-year: $357/month for $30,000 loan. Graduated: 10 years (starts lower, increases). Income-driven: 20-25 years. If you pay extra: Can be 5-7 years. Interest paid varies from $13,000-$20,000+ depending on plan.

Q: Are there loans for graduate school?
A: Yes. Unsubsidized Direct Loans: Up to $20,500/year. Grad PLUS Loans: Up to cost of attendance. Perkins Loans: Up to $8,000/year (limited availability). Private loans: Various lenders and terms. Graduate students have higher borrowing limits than undergrads.
